Abstract—The loss of a close loved one deprives individuals not
only of a physical presence but of an entire ecosystem of emo-
tional guidance, comfort, and personalised interaction. Existing
digital archives—photographs, messages, and voice recordings—
remain static and passive, offering no interactive medium for
continued engagement. This paper proposes MemoryBridge, an
AI-based conversational system that learns from multimodal
personal data including text messages, voice notes, and be-
havioural patterns to simulate emotionally familiar interactions
with deceased individuals. The system delivers text, voice, and
visually animated responses that preserve the linguistic style,
tonal patterns, and emotional characteristics of the lost person.
Built on a locally-served Large Language Model with structured
persona prompting, an XTTS v2 voice synthesis module, a
FAISS-backed memory-augmented dialogue manager, a seven-
class emotion-aware response pipeline, and a real-time 3D avatar
with lip synchronisation, MemoryBridge provides contextually
sensitive interactions that are especially meaningful during
significant life moments such as birthdays, anniversaries, and
personal milestones. Ethical safeguards including consent-by-
design, representational humility, crisis interception, and AI-
generation transparency are embedded at every layer of the
system. A deepfake text detection module further safeguards
interaction quality. The paper details the system architecture,
prototype implementation, ethical framework, and evaluation
methodology.

I. INTRODUCTION
Bereavement is among the most profound psychological
experiences a human being can endure. Beyond the immediate
grief of physical loss, individuals mourn the cessation of a
unique communicative relationship shaped by years of shared
language, emotional cadence, and personal history. A parent’s
advice, a partner’s reassurance, a friend’s humour: these are
not simply the content of communication, but its irreplaceable
texture.
Contemporary digital technology has transformed the land-
scape of personal data accumulation. The average individual
leaves behind tens of thousands of text messages, voice
recordings, social media posts, and video clips—a rich, largely
untapped archive of linguistic and behavioural identity. Yet
these records remain passive artefacts. They can be read or
listened to, but they cannot respond, contextualise, or adapt to
new circumstances.
This gap motivates the present work. We propose Memo-
ryBridge, an AI-based conversational system that processes
multimodal personal data to construct and maintain an inter-
active, emotionally adaptive persona of a deceased individual.
The system enables text, voice, and avatar-based dialogue that
mirrors the linguistic style, emotional register, and personal
knowledge base of the loved one.
The ethical dimension of such a system is non-trivial.
Digital afterlife technologies carry significant risks: questions
of personal identity, posthumous data ownership, religious
and cultural sensitivities, and the potential for grief prolon-
gation demand careful governance [9]. MemoryBridge ad-
dresses these risks through a consent-by-design framework
and a transparency-first interaction model [1]. The system
also incorporates a deepfake text authentication layer [10] to
ensure that AI-generated responses are never misrepresented
as human-authored communications, and a keyword-based
crisis interception framework to safeguard psychologically
vulnerable users.
The key contributions of this paper are:
• A multimodal personal data ingestion and processing
pipeline for constructing a deceased individual’s conver-
sational persona.
• A locally-served LLM architecture using structured per-
sona prompting [3] for persona-faithful, emotionally re-
sponsive dialogue generation, with a planned LoRA fine-
tuning pathway for production deployment.
• A voice synthesis module replicating the deceased’s vocal
characteristics using XTTS v2 [11], supporting zero-shot
voice cloning from minimal audio samples.
• A FAISS-backed memory architecture [8] with Short-
Term Memory (STM) and Long-Term Memory (LTM)
for persistent persona retention across sessions.
• An emotion-aware response engine classifying seven
emotional states and contextualising interactions based
on significant life events.
• A real-time 3D avatar module with audio-driven lip
synchronisation via the Web Audio API and Three.js.
• A deepfake text detection module [10] ensuring AI-
generation transparency for users.
• A keyword-based crisis interception framework that by-
passes LLM processing to deliver immediate safety re-
sponses during high-risk interactions.
• A consent-by-design ethical framework [1], [9] govern-
ing data collection, persona deployment, and interaction
boundaries.
• Comprehensive evaluation across persona fidelity, emo-
tional alignment, voice naturalness, memory retrieval
quality, and user experience metrics.

II. RELATED WORK
A. Digital Immortality and Posthumous AI Personas
The concept of digital immortality has emerged as a mul-
tidisciplinary research domain [1]. Syarova et al. identify
four foundational ethical pillars: data privacy, representational
authenticity, dynamic consent, and emotional safeguards for
bereaved relatives. Their work reviews commercial implemen-
tations including Replika, HereAfter AI, Eterni.me, and Deep
Nostalgia, identifying significant gaps in unified governance
standards.
Complementing this technical survey, Perov et al. [9] pro-
vide a philosophical analysis of digital afterlife, introducing
precise distinctions between a digital imitation (a static repro-
duction recombining existing data) and a digital personality
(a system employing predictive analytics to model behaviour
in new situations). Their work highlights three central ethical
tensions: the impossibility of freezing a morally evolving
human identity into a statistical model; the ambiguity of data
ownership for deceased persons who left no pre-mortem con-
sent; and the diversity of religious and cultural attitudes toward
posthumous representation. MemoryBridge explicitly adopts
the conservative position of digital imitation as recommended
by [9].
B. Interactive Digital Avatar Platforms
Awasare et al. [2] present AfterLife Connect, integrating
LLaMA 3 and Mistral 7B for dialogue, Tacotron 2 and F5-TTS
for voice synthesis, and StyleGAN3 with FOMM for facial
animation, deployed on Google Cloud Platform with sub-
second latency. A proof-of-concept Einstein avatar achieved
90% persona consistency, 94% voice likeness, and SSIM
of 0.82, establishing key benchmarks for interactive digital
persona systems.
C. Voice Cloning and Text-to-Speech
Rajkumar et al. [7] present VoiceChat, a deep learning-based
chat application converting text messages into personalised
audio using voice cloning. Their system demonstrates that
a small number of voice samples suffices to capture an
individual’s accent, inflections, and speech patterns. The four-
step pipeline—voice recording, vocal pattern analysis, voice
cloning, and synthesised audio integration—directly informs
MemoryBridge’s voice module. Their evaluation criteria cov-
ering naturalness, intelligibility, prosody, linguistic accuracy,
and Mean Opinion Score (MOS) provide the evaluation tem-
plate for MemoryBridge’s voice component. MemoryBridge
adopts XTTS v2 [11], a multilingual zero-shot TTS model em-
ploying a flow-based architecture for expressive voice cloning
from minimal reference audio.
D. Memory-Augmented Dialogue Systems
Huang et al. [8] present REMIND (Recall-Enhanced Mem-
ory Integration for Natural Language Dialogue Systems), a
lightweight modular framework integrating STM for session-
specific recall and LTM for persistent knowledge retention.
REMIND employs hybrid retrieval combining vector-based
similarity search, hierarchical grouping, iterative query re-
finement, and keyword filtering. Evaluated on the MemoRA
dataset, LTM Retrieve achieved relevance scores of mean
8.42 and median 9.0 out of 10 graded by GPT-4o-mini,
outperforming MemGPT on long-term retrieval coherence.
MemoryBridge adopts this STM/LTM pattern for cross-session
persona consistency, implementing the vector storage layer
using FAISS [14] with Sentence-BERT embeddings.
E. Emotionally Adaptive AI and Personalised Intelligence
Varma et al. [3] introduce Artificial Personalized Intelli-
gence, comparing LoRA fine-tuning of LLaMA-3 8B against
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) for emotionally per-
sonalised response generation. Using a custom dataset of 30
participants across six universal emotions, fine-tuning signif-
icantly outperforms RAG (Disgust: Pearson r = 0.890 vs.
0.821; average cosine similarity 0.76; 44% of predictions ex-
ceeding 80% similarity). RAG underperforms due to LLaMA-
3’s adversarial safety training biasing outputs toward neutral
responses—a key finding motivating MemoryBridge’s planned
LoRA fine-tuning pathway.
F. LLM-Based Persona Generation
Min and Jeong [6] demonstrate that LLMs can generate
contextually rich, persona-faithful interactions from diverse
personality configurations in a VR-based public speaking
Q&A practice system. In a user study with 20 participants,
LLM-generated questions were rated higher in quality (avg.
rank 4.14) than human-generated questions (4.85), with 13
out of 20 participants selecting an LLM-generated question
as their first choice. The system achieved SUS of 75.0 and
UEQ Stimulation M = 1.975, validating LLM-based persona
generation for high-fidelity interactive applications. Memory-
Bridge draws on this persona engineering methodology for its
structured system prompt design.
G. Deepfake Text Detection
Chong et al. [10] propose a deepfake text detection system
combining fine-tuned BERT embeddings, emoji2vec embed-
dings, linguistic features, sentiment features via RoBERTa,
and a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) classifier. On the en-
hanced TweepFake dataset of 21,730 tweets, their model
achieves 88.3% accuracy and F1 scores of 88.1%–88.3%,
outperforming a fine-tuned RoBERTa baseline of 87.6%. Key
findings: machine-generated text exhibits higher neutral senti-
ment proportions, lower perplexity for modern models, fewer
user mentions and URLs, and distinct part-of-speech (POS)
patterns. These characteristics inform MemoryBridge’s AI-
generation transparency module.
III. METHODOLOGY
A. System Overview
MemoryBridge is a modular, privacy-first conversational AI
system comprising seven principal modules: (1) Multimodal
Data Ingestion and Processing; (2) Persona Construction and
LLM Serving; (3) Voice Synthesis and Cloning; (4) Memory-
Augmented Dialogue Manager; (5) Emotion-Aware Response
Engine; (6) Real-Time 3D Avatar and Lip Synchronisation;
and (7) AI Transparency, Crisis Interception, and Ethical
Governance Layer.
The current prototype is deployed as a locally-hosted,
edge-AI system. This architecture prioritises data privacy and
enables zero-latency data access during inference without
reliance on external cloud services. A cloud-hosted production
pathway (Google Cloud Platform with Kubernetes orchestra-
tion) is planned for scalable deployment.
B. Multimodal Data Ingestion and Processing
1) Data Sources: MemoryBridge ingests three primary data
modalities:
• Text Data: Chat message exports (WhatsApp, iMessage,
email), social media posts, and written notes, forming the
primary training corpus for linguistic style replication.
• Voice Data: Voice notes, phone call recordings, and video
audio tracks. XTTS v2 [11] supports zero-shot voice
cloning from a minimum of approximately 6 seconds of
clean reference audio, making it suitable for cases with
limited recorded material.
• Behavioural
Data: Communication timing patterns,
frequently discussed topics, emotional response trig-
gers, preferred vocabulary, relationship-specific linguistic
markers, and personality profile indicators (e.g., MBTI-
style dimensions) used to calibrate the persona system
prompt.

2) Text Data Processing Pipeline: Raw message exports
are preprocessed through the following steps:
1) Cleaning: Removal of timestamps, sender tags, media
placeholders, and system messages.
2) Speaker Diarization: Separation of the deceased’s mes-
sages using Deepgram Nova-2, as employed in [3].
3) Conversation Formatting: Construction of question-
answer pairs and multi-turn dialogue sequences for fine-
tuning.
4) Embedding and Clustering: Semantic clustering via
cosine similarity using all-MiniLM-L6-v2 embed-
dings to identify emotional patterns, recurring themes,
and vocabulary signatures.
5) Persona Profile Extraction: Automated extraction of
vocabulary range, sentence length distribution, emoji
usage, greeting and closing patterns, topic preferences,
and personality indicators, analogous to persona charac-
terisation in [6] and [5].
3) Voice Data Processing: Audio recordings are processed
through a four-step pipeline informed by [7]: (1) voice sample
registration; (2) vocal pattern analysis via deep neural net-
works; (3) voice cloning using XTTS v2 [11]; and (4) inte-
gration of synthesised audio with generated text responses.
C. Persona Construction and LLM Serving
1) Prototype: Ollama-Served LLaMA-3: The current proto-
type serves LLaMA-3 8B locally via Ollama [13], pre-warmed
at startup to minimise first-response latency. Persona fidelity is
achieved through structured system prompt engineering rather
than weight-level fine-tuning in this prototype phase.

tion deployment, the model will be fine-tuned using Low-

3) Persona System Prompt Engineering: A structured sys-
tem prompt encodes the deceased individual’s persona profile,
covering: relationship-specific address patterns, known opin-
ions and values, characteristic humour and rhetorical patterns,
emotional response tendencies, and personality dimensions
derived from the behavioural data pipeline. This approach is
inspired by the LLM-based persona generation methodology
of [6] and the ConvoGen characterisation approach of [5].
The prompt is dynamically assembled at inference time by
combining the static persona profile with retrieved memory
context and detected emotional state.
D. Voice Synthesis and Cloning
MemoryBridge employs XTTS v2 [11] for voice synthesis,
a massively multilingual zero-shot TTS model that replicates
a speaker’s voice characteristics from a short reference audio
clip without requiring speaker-specific training. The model’s
flow-based architecture enables expressive, naturalistically
prosodic speech output across diverse emotional registers.
The voice pipeline follows the four-stage structure described
in [7]: (1) voice sample registration from uploaded recordings;
(2) vocal pattern extraction; (3) zero-shot voice cloning via
XTTS v2; and (4) integration of synthesised audio with the
response pipeline and 3D avatar system.
In observed prototype performance, XTTS v2 achieves a
Real-Time Factor (RTF) of approximately 2.7–3.6, yielding
synthesis times of 6–15 seconds per sentence on CPU-only
inference. GPU acceleration is expected to reduce RTF below
1.0 in production. Voice output is evaluated using MOS,
intelligibility, prosody, linguistic accuracy, adaptability, and
speaker similarity, following the criteria of [7].
E. Memory-Augmented Dialogue Manager
MemoryBridge adopts the REMIND framework [8] for
managing conversational context across sessions, imple-
mented using FAISS [14] as the vector store backend with
all-MiniLM-L6-v2 (Sentence-BERT) embeddings.
1) Short-Term Memory: Manages session-specific conver-
sational data as a temporary buffer, maintaining a sliding
window:
CSTM(t) = {ct−w+1, . . . , ct}
(1)
where ct is the conversation at time t and w is the window size.
Vector-based similarity search retrieves relevant interactions
sorted by timestamp for chronological coherence.
2) Long-Term Memory: Provides persistent storage by hi-
erarchically organising conversations into topic-based groups
via the FAISS index. At each session start, the FAISS index
is loaded from disk and cross-checked against the structured
fact store for consistency. Retrieval is a multi-step iterative
process: top-k groups are ranked by cosine similarity to the
query vector, and the top-3 most relevant past interactions are
injected into the system prompt before response generation [8].
3) Hybrid Retrieval: STM and LTM are queried concur-
rently and merged into a unified prompt [8]:
P = gcombine(RelSTM, RLTM, q)
(2)
which is passed to the LLM for response generation.

G. Real-Time 3D Avatar and Lip Synchronisation
MemoryBridge renders an interactive 3D avatar to provide a
visual presence for the simulated persona. The avatar module
comprises two sub-components: scene rendering and audio-
driven animation.
1) Scene Rendering: The frontend constructs a Three.js
scene with ambient and directional lighting and loads the
persona’s 3D model from a GLB/GLTF file. The scene is
served via the Flask backend and rendered natively in the
browser without external plugins.
2) Audio-Driven Lip Synchronisation: Upon receiving syn-
thesised audio from XTTS v2, the frontend captures the audio
stream using the Web Audio API AnalyserNode. The
analyser computes per-frame RMS amplitude and frequency
data, which are mapped directly to the jaw bone rotation of
the avatar’s skeletal rig. Interpolation between frames ensures
smooth, naturalistic mouth movement that tracks the temporal
envelope of the synthesised speech. For avatars rigged with
facial blendshapes, the system additionally maps frequency
bands to standard viseme morph targets (e.g., mouthOpen,
viseme_A).
This amplitude-to-jaw mapping provides a lightweight, real-
time synchronisation that operates within the constraints of
client-side CPU inference without requiring a backend viseme
extraction pipeline.
H. AI Transparency, Crisis Interception, and Ethical Gover-
nance Layer
1) Deepfake Text Detection: MemoryBridge incorporates a
deepfake text detection module based on [10] to ensure users
are never misled about the AI-generated nature of responses.
The module analyses generated text using perplexity scoring
via GPT-2, sentiment polarity via RoBERTa, and linguistic
feature analysis via BERT with an MLP, flagging responses for
transparency disclosures. Key detection signals [10] include:
• Perplexity: Modern LLM outputs exhibit characteristi-
cally low perplexity scores.
• Sentiment: Machine-generated text shows higher neutral
sentiment proportions than human-written text.
• POS Patterns: AI text uses higher NOUN, PRON, DET,
and ADP ratios; lower PROPN and ADV ratios.
All responses are labelled with a visible AI disclosure indi-
cator, operationalising the representational humility principle
of [1], [9].
2) Crisis Interception Framework: Prior to every LLM
inference call, the user’s input is scanned for high-risk crisis
keywords (e.g., “suicide,” “self-harm,” “die”). Upon detec-
tion, the LLM processing pipeline is immediately bypassed
and a hard-coded, professionally reviewed safety response
is returned, directing the user to seek professional mental
health support. This deterministic interception ensures that no
hallucinated or inappropriate AI-generated content is delivered
during a potential crisis, regardless of the LLM’s output. The
interception event is logged for monitoring and subsequent
review.

B. Data Onboarding Interface
An onboarding interface guides the user through four stages:
(1) Consent Configuration: selection of permitted interaction
modes, data retention duration, and user access controls, all
logged immutably consistent with [1], [9]; (2) Data Upload:
chat export uploads and voice file ingestion following the
pipeline described in [7]; (3) Profile Verification: user review
of extracted persona traits and personality dimensions before
persona activation; and (4) Legal Acknowledgement: clear
disclosure that MemoryBridge creates a digital imitation and
not a digital personality [9], with no claim to subjectivity,
consciousness, or autonomous agency.
C. Deployment Architecture
The current prototype is deployed as a locally-hosted edge-
AI system on Ubuntu/WSL, serving all inference workloads
on-device. This design eliminates network latency for LLM
inference and ensures that personal data never leaves the user’s
machine during the prototype phase. The Flask server handles
HTTP endpoints for the chat pipeline, audio file serving, and
static frontend delivery. Audio files are automatically purged
from disk after one hour to limit storage footprint.
A cloud-based production deployment following the mi-
croservices architecture of [2] with Kubernetes orchestration
on Google Cloud Platform is planned as a future milestone.
D. Hardware-Aware Graceful Degradation
Given the multiple concurrent ML models (LLM, TTS,
emotion classifier, embedding model), initialisation of the
memory and emotion modules is wrapped in exception-
handling blocks. If an out-of-memory (OOM) condition is
detected at startup, these modules degrade gracefully while
the core Flask–Ollama–XTTS voice pipeline continues to
serve responses. This design ensures system availability under
resource-constrained hardware conditions.
V. EVALUATION FRAMEWORK
A. Evaluation Dimensions
MemoryBridge is evaluated across six primary dimensions.
1) Persona Fidelity:
• Cosine Similarity: Between generated responses and
held-out messages from the deceased (target: ≥0.76 [3]).
• Perplexity: Of generated text under a reference language
model trained on the deceased’s messages.
• Human Evaluation: Blind A/B testing by individuals
who knew the deceased, using a 1 to 10 persona authen-
ticity scale.
2) Emotional Alignment: Adapting MSE and Pearson Cor-
relation metrics from [3] to measure alignment of generated
emotional tone with expected responses across seven emotion
categories (Joy, Sadness, Fear, Anger, Surprise, Disgust, Neu-
tral).
3) Memory Retrieval Quality: Adapting the REMIND eval-
uation methodology [8]: GPT-4o-mini as an LLM judge for
relevance and coherence scoring from 1 to 10; ROUGE-L
recall for session-specific retrieval; and KDE visualisations
for score distribution analysis. Additional attention is given
to FAISS index–fact store consistency, quantified by the mis-
match rate between stored embeddings and the structured
persona fact store.
4) Voice Naturalness: MOS from human raters for synthe-
sised voice, and Speaker Similarity score between generated
audio and reference samples, following the TTS evaluation
criteria of [7]. RTF is additionally reported as an infrastructure
performance metric.
5) Avatar Naturalness: Human perceptual evaluation of lip-
sync accuracy: time-to-mouth alignment offset and a 1–10
naturalness rating for jaw movement fidelity relative to the
synthesised audio envelope.
6) User Experience and Psychological Impact:
• System Usability Scale scores (target: ≥75 [6]).
• User Experience Questionnaire across six dimensions:
Attractiveness,
Perspicuity,
Efficiency,
Dependability,
Stimulation, and Novelty [6].
• Validated grief questionnaires (Prolonged Grief Disorder
scale, PG-13) administered pre- and post-use.
• Qualitative interviews on perceived comfort, appropriate-
ness, and emotional impact.
B. Evaluation Protocol
A controlled user study with two participant groups:
Group A—bereaved individuals who have experienced loss
within the past two years, using MemoryBridge over a four-
week period; and Group B—individuals who knew the de-
ceased, assessing persona fidelity without direct grief bias.
All participants provide informed consent; the protocol is re-
viewed by an institutional ethics board; psychological support
resources are available throughout.
VI. ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS
Ethical responsibility is central to MemoryBridge’s design,
informed by the comprehensive analyses of [1] and [9].
A. Consent-by-Design
Pre-mortem consent is strongly preferred: individuals may
configure their MemoryBridge persona while alive, specify-
ing permitted use cases, interaction boundaries, authorised
users, and expiry conditions. Where pre-mortem consent is
unavailable, post-mortem consent must be obtained from des-
ignated data executors [9]. All consent records are logged
immutably [1].
B. Digital Imitation vs. Digital Personality
Following [9], MemoryBridge implements a digital imita-
tion rather than a digital personality. The distinction is en-
forced architecturally: the system is constrained to reproduce
attested linguistic patterns and does not generate opinions or
beliefs absent from the training data.
C. Representational Humility and AI Disclosure
Every session begins with a visible disclaimer that Mem-
oryBridge simulates—but does not replicate—the identity of
the deceased, with no claim to consciousness or autonomous
agency [9]. The deepfake text detection module [10] provides
a technical implementation of AI-generation transparency.
D. Crisis Interception and Psychological Safeguards
Informed by research in [1] identifying a 22% incidence
of prolonged grief disorder alongside a 68% reduction in
complicated grief symptoms, MemoryBridge implements a
layered psychological safety framework comprising:
• Crisis keyword interception (pre-inference): User input
is scanned for high-risk terms before every LLM call.
Upon detection, LLM processing is bypassed and a hard-
coded professional safety response is delivered immedi-
ately.
• Session duration limits (default 30 minutes) with grad-
uated warnings for extended usage.
• Automatic referral to grief counselling resources upon
session termination or crisis detection.
• Mandatory cooling-off periods after significant anniver-
sary dates.
• Minor protection: Access by individuals under 18 years
of age requires parental or guardian consent and is subject
to additional psychological monitoring [1].
E. Personal Data and Posthumous Ownership
All data is encrypted at rest using AES-256 and in transit
using TLS 1.3. In the current prototype, model weights and
FAISS indices are stored in isolated, user-specific local direc-
tories. Anomaly detection for unauthorised access is planned
for the production deployment [1].
F. Right to Erasure
Any authorised user may permanently delete all associated
data, FAISS indices, and model artefacts at any time, consis-
tent with GDPR Article 17 [1].
G. Religious and Cultural Sensitivity
Perov et al. [9] note that attitudes toward posthumous
digital representation vary fundamentally across religious and
cultural traditions. MemoryBridge provides region-specific in-
teraction guides and recommends cultural consultation before
deployment in communities with strong traditional mourning
practices.

B. Prototype Observations
Initial prototype runs confirm successful integration of
all seven modules. XTTS v2 generates voice output with
an RTF of approximately 2.7–3.6 on CPU-only inference,
yielding synthesis times of 6–15 seconds per sentence. GPU-
accelerated inference is expected to bring the RTF below
1.0. The FAISS memory module initialises correctly when
the vector index and structured fact store are synchronised; a
mismatch detection mechanism has been implemented to flag
and log index–store inconsistencies at server startup. The 3D
avatar renders in-browser with smooth jaw animation driven by
the Web Audio API analyser, with subjective visual coherence
rated positively in informal testing.
C. Limitations
LLM
Fine-Tuning
Gap. The current prototype uses
a
general-purpose
Ollama-served
LLaMA-3
8B
without
persona-specific LoRA fine-tuning. While structured persona
prompting achieves plausible fidelity, fine-tuned weights are
expected to yield significantly higher cosine similarity and
emotional alignment scores [3].
TTS Latency on CPU. XTTS v2 on CPU-only hardware
produces an RTF of 2.7–3.6, making real-time voice synthe-
sis infeasible without GPU acceleration. This is a hardware
constraint of the prototype environment and does not reflect
production performance.
FAISS Index Synchronisation. The vector index can be-
come desynchronised from the structured fact store across
sessions, resulting in a mismatch between indexed embeddings
and retrievable facts. A startup-time consistency check has
been implemented; a transactional write mechanism is planned
for production.
Data Sufficiency. Persona fidelity is constrained by the
volume and diversity of available personal data.
Memory Coherence at Scale. As noted in [8], LLM
variability in grouping quality can degrade LTM retrieval
coherence over long interaction histories.
Hallucination Risk. LLMs can generate plausible but fac-
tually incorrect statements about the deceased. The deepfake
detection layer [10] provides partial mitigation by flagging
low-perplexity, high-neutral-sentiment outputs for review.
Persona Authenticity Limits. The system operates as a
digital imitation [9]. Generating responses to entirely novel
scenarios risks misrepresenting the deceased’s views.
Cultural
and
Temporal
Drift. Communication style
evolves over time and varies across cultures [9]. Training data
from different life periods may contain contradictory linguistic
patterns.
Grief Prolongation Risk. While digital memorials can
reduce complicated grief symptoms by up to 68% [1], they
also carry a 22% risk of prolonged grief disorder.
VIII. CONCLUSION
This paper presents MemoryBridge, an AI-based conver-
sational system addressing the profound communicative void
left by bereavement. The prototype integrates a locally-served
LLaMA-3 8B with structured persona prompting, XTTS v2
zero-shot voice synthesis [11], FAISS-backed memory man-
agement [8], seven-class emotion-aware response genera-
tion [12], real-time 3D avatar lip synchronisation, a keyword-
based crisis interception framework, deepfake text trans-
parency [10], and a consent-by-design ethical framework [1],
[9].
The system explicitly commits to operating as a digital
imitation—a bounded, data-grounded reconstruction maintain-
ing representational humility—rather than a digital personality
claiming autonomous identity [9].
Future work will focus on: production-grade LoRA fine-
tuning of LLaMA-3 8B [3]; GPU-accelerated TTS inference
to achieve sub-second voice latency; longitudinal evaluation of
psychological impact using validated grief scales; FAISS index
transactional consistency guarantees; multimodal avatar im-
provements using blendshape-rigged models; federated learn-
ing for privacy-preserving personalisation; cloud deployment
on GCP with Kubernetes orchestration; and cross-cultural
adaptation of interaction frameworks [9].
